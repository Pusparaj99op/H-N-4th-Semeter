Practical No. 01 – Study of Network Topologies

Aim

To study and understand different types of computer network topologies along with their characteristics, advantages, and disadvantages.

Theory

A network topology is the physical or logical arrangement of nodes and connections in a network. Choosing the right topology affects cost, performance, scalability, and fault tolerance.

1. Bus Topology

All devices are connected to a single shared cable called the bus or backbone. Data is sent in both directions and received by all devices; only the intended recipient accepts it. A terminator at each end prevents signal reflection.

  • Advantages: Easy and inexpensive to install; requires less cable than other topologies.
  • Disadvantages: A break in the main cable brings down the entire network; performance degrades as more devices are added; limited cable length.
  • Used in: Early Ethernet (10Base2 coaxial) networks.

2. Star Topology

All devices connect to a central hub or switch. The central device manages all data transmission. This is the most widely used LAN topology today.

  • Advantages: Easy to install, manage, and troubleshoot; failure of one device does not affect others; easy to add/remove devices.
  • Disadvantages: If the central hub/switch fails, the entire network goes down; requires more cable than bus topology.
  • Used in: Modern Ethernet LANs with managed switches.

3. Ring Topology

Each device is connected to exactly two neighbouring devices forming a closed loop. Data travels in one direction using a token-passing mechanism; a device transmits only when it holds the token.

  • Advantages: Equal access for all devices; performs well under heavy traffic load.
  • Disadvantages: Failure of any single node can break the ring; difficult to diagnose faults; adding/removing devices disrupts the network.
  • Used in: Token Ring (IEEE 802.5), FDDI networks.

4. Mesh Topology

Every device is connected to every other device directly (full mesh). Partial mesh connects only some devices. Number of connections in full mesh = n(n−1)/2.

  • Advantages: Highly reliable and fault-tolerant; no single point of failure; data can be routed around failures.
  • Disadvantages: Very expensive; complex installation and configuration; high cabling cost.
  • Used in: WANs, backbone networks, military communication.

5. Tree (Hierarchical) Topology

A combination of star and bus topologies. Multiple star-configured networks are connected to a common bus backbone. Devices are arranged in a hierarchical parent-child relationship.

  • Advantages: Easily scalable; fault isolation is straightforward; high-priority devices can be located near the root.
  • Disadvantages: Failure of the backbone affects the entire network; can become complex as it grows.
  • Used in: Corporate networks, campus networks.

6. Hybrid Topology

A combination of two or more different topologies (e.g., Star-Bus, Star-Ring). Organizations use hybrid topologies to leverage the advantages of multiple types.

  • Advantages: Highly flexible and scalable; best features of component topologies can be utilized.
  • Disadvantages: Complex design; expensive to install and manage.
  • Used in: Large enterprise and university campus networks.

Apparatus / Requirements

  • Computer System with Windows OS
  • Cisco Packet Tracer / GNS3 (network simulation software)
  • Microsoft Visio or Draw.io (topology diagrams)
  • Pen and paper for manual sketches

Procedure

  1. Open Cisco Packet Tracer on the computer.
  2. Create a new project workspace (File → New).
  3. Bus Topology: Place 5 PCs from the device panel. Connect them all in a line to a single hub simulating a bus. Add labels.
  4. Star Topology: Place 1 Switch and 5 PCs. Connect each PC to the switch using a straight-through cable. Label all devices.
  5. Ring Topology: Place 5 PCs. Connect PC1→PC2→PC3→PC4→PC5→PC1 forming a closed loop.
  6. Mesh Topology: Place 4 PCs. Draw a direct connection from every PC to every other PC (6 connections total).
  7. Tree Topology: Create two star sub-networks (each with 3 PCs and a switch), then connect both switches to a root switch.
  8. Hybrid Topology: Combine the star and bus topologies by connecting the star switch to a bus backbone.
  9. Label all devices and connections in every diagram.
  10. Save each topology as a separate Packet Tracer file.
  11. Note down the advantages, disadvantages, and real-world use case for each topology.

Result

All six types of computer network topologies — Bus, Star, Ring, Mesh, Tree, and Hybrid — were successfully studied, drawn, and simulated using Cisco Packet Tracer. Their structure, working principles, advantages, disadvantages, and use cases were understood and documented.
